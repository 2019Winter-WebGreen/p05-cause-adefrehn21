What is Pediatric Cancer?


Pediatric Cancer is a term used to describe cancers that occur between birth and 15 years of age. They are rare, and often times even more difficult to treat than adult cancers in the way that they grow and spread, how they are treat, and how their bodies respond to treatment. Some of the more common types of childhood cnacer is leukemia, lymphoma, neuroblastoma, retinoblastoma, Wilms tumor, and other cancers of the brain, bone, and soft tissue.

Some Childhood Cancer Statistics:


  • Childhood Cancer is the second leading cause of children from ages 1 to 14, after accidents.
  • Around 1,180 children 15 and younger are expected to pass away from cancer in 2018.
  • Current treatment plans have made it possible for 80% of children to survive 5 or more years now.
  • 1 in 285 children will be diagnosed with cancer.
  • More than 95% of childhood cancer survivors will have a significant health-related issue by the time they are 45 years of age.
  • 43 children per day or 15,780 children per year are expected to be diagnosed with cancer.
  • The average cost of a stay in a hospital for a child with cancer is $40,000.
  • Only 4% of federal government cancer research funding goes to study pediatric cancer.
